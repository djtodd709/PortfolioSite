Volumetric Cloud Experiments

May 2020

For my dissertation at Newcastle University, I investigated various methods for improving performance when rendering volumetric clouds. My paper won the School of Computing prize for an outstanding dissertation that year, and can be read here.

Inspired by the work of Nathan Vos and Andrew Schneider on Horizon: Zero Dawn, I created a program to render volumetric clouds using C++ and OpenGL, so that I could invesitigate how different parameters affected the performance of a notoriously computationally intensive task. Using the Mean Squared Error and Structural Similarity Index Measurement as a metric for how similar captured frames were to frames with maximum settings, I was able to measure the impact of:

  • Raymarching Step Size (Camera to Cloud)
  • Raymarching Step Size (Cloud to Light Source)
  • Step Optimization (step size proportional to density sampled)
  • Fragment vs Compute Shader

Alongside other more structural changes designed to test which shapes of clouds were the least intensive to render. The video below demonstrates the majority of the available settings.

What I'd do next time

Add more parameters, and further refine the existing ones. Step optimization was very successful at improving the frame rate, and I definitely think there were more savings to be made by tinkering with the algorithm I created. It would also be interesting to see the results on more powerful hardware, this project took place during lockdown in 2020, so access to powerful computers wasn't available. Stronger hardware would give an extra degree of precision to results since different settings would presumably have much larger FPS differences, and the impact of other background processes would be reduced.
